# 📝 Reflection

## 1️⃣ How does the gameplay teach the concept?

The primary concept taught by this game is **Context is Everything**. Rather than explaining the concept through text, players learn it through direct interaction.

At the beginning of the game, players manually guide citizens to the correct buildings while **Safaid Khan** observes and learns from each example. Over time, Safaid Khan begins making decisions independently.

Initially, he relies on simple patterns. For example, if a citizen is carrying books, he may assume that person belongs at the school. Later, the game introduces more complex situations, such as:

> A student carrying books who is also injured.

If Safaid Khan focuses only on the obvious clue, he makes the wrong decision. The player then demonstrates the correct action, allowing him to improve.

The lesson emerges naturally through gameplay:

- One clue is not always enough.
- Good decisions require understanding the full situation.
- AI performs better when it considers context instead of isolated signals.

> **Key Takeaway:** Context matters more than individual clues.

---

## 2️⃣ Where did the game slide toward a quiz?

During the early design phase, the game resembled a simple sorting activity.

Players would:

1. See an icon.
2. Match it to a building.
3. Receive immediate feedback.

This felt more like a quiz than a game.

To solve this issue, several changes were made:

### Removed

- ❌ Multiple-choice questions
- ❌ "Correct!" popups
- ❌ "Wrong!" screens
- ❌ Quiz-style answer checking

### Added

- ✅ Natural in-game consequences
- ✅ Citizen confusion
- ✅ Longer queues
- ✅ Reduced Town Mood
- ✅ Situations containing multiple clues

Instead of matching symbols, players must evaluate the complete situation before deciding.

> The learning now happens through the mechanics themselves rather than through explicit testing.

---

## 3️⃣ Which AI concept(s) did you use, and why?

### 🎯 Primary Concept: Context is Everything

The entire gameplay loop revolves around teaching Safaid Khan to consider multiple pieces of information before making decisions.

Players repeatedly discover that:

- Surface-level clues can be misleading.
- Multiple factors must be considered together.
- Better context leads to better outcomes.

### 🤖 Supporting Concept: Learning from Examples

The game also introduces the idea of **Learning from Examples**.

Safaid Khan:

- Starts with limited knowledge.
- Watches player decisions.
- Learns from demonstrations.
- Gradually becomes more independent.

This creates the feeling of training an AI assistant without requiring players to understand technical AI terminology.

> By the end of the game, players experience *why* context matters instead of simply being told that it matters.

# 🔄 Examples of How I Redirected the AI

## Redirect Prompt #1

> The game is becoming too much like a quiz. Remove any multiple-choice or correct/incorrect screens. The lesson should live in the gameplay mechanic, with natural consequences instead of quiz feedback.

---

## Redirect Prompt #2

> Make Safaid Khan a companion that players enjoy training instead of just an AI system. Give him visible progression and make players feel emotionally responsible for helping him learn.

---

## Redirect Prompt #3

> Please simplify the scope so the final game is polished instead of overcomplicated. Keep only the essential mechanics that teach the concept while remaining realistic as a single HTML file built with HTML, CSS, and JavaScript.

# 🎓 What I Learned

This project taught me that designing an educational game is very different from creating a quiz.

The biggest challenge was ensuring that the AI concept emerged naturally from the player's actions rather than being explained through text.

Working with Claude was highly iterative:

- Refining the design repeatedly
- Removing unnecessary features
- Simplifying mechanics
- Focusing on a single polished gameplay loop

I also learned that effective prompting goes beyond generating code.

Good prompting involves:

- Giving clear feedback
- Iterating on ideas
- Redirecting the AI when needed
- Guiding design decisions toward better outcomes

> **Final Insight:** AI is most powerful when used as a collaborative design partner rather than simply a code generator.

---

## 🚀 Project Summary

**Train Safaid Khan** demonstrates how AI concepts can be taught through gameplay rather than quizzes. By training an AI companion and observing the consequences of context-aware versus context-blind decisions, players develop an intuitive understanding of why **Context is Everything**.
